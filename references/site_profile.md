## Section 1 - What Inkstone Is

Inkstone is a student-run digital publication for Chinese students to explore identity, society, and creativity through written work, visual art, video, and other narrative forms. Its public mission describes the site as a space for Chinese students to tell stories "on our own terms" rather than being reduced to geopolitical talking points, stereotypes, or narratives filtered through outside observers.

The site is made by and for Chinese students broadly understood: students in Chinese public schools, students on international tracks, American-born Chinese students, and Chinese students abroad. It is not only a student philosophy blog, although the current archive is philosophy-heavy. Inkstone frames itself as an open platform and "literary and artistic salon" for authentic creative voices of Chinese students worldwide.

What makes Inkstone distinctive is the mix of identity mission and student intellectual production. The current archive includes ethics olympiad commentaries, beginner-friendly philosophy explainers, and AI-assisted philosophy videos, but the about page also explicitly invites personal essays, literature, photography, AI filmmaking, artistic projects, social work records, meaningful initiatives, and product-like projects. Agents should treat Inkstone as a young creative-intellectual platform, not as a generic school newspaper or a purely academic philosophy newsletter.

The site's three identity pillars should be understood in practice as:

- **Authenticity**: Raw, honest, sometimes imperfect student expression that foregrounds personality and lived perspective rather than polished institutional voice.
- **Creativity**: Openness to unusual formats and ideas, including written argument, AI film, visual art, speculative work, and projects that do not fit a standard magazine category.
- **Vibrance**: A desire to show Chinese students as alive, varied, and self-defining rather than as abstract representatives of a geopolitical category.

## Section 2 - Current Content

The site currently skews philosophy-heavy, but the mission is broader. Future content may include art, artistic commentary, student social projects, cultural work, and other formats. When promoting the site as a whole, represent the mission, not just the current philosophy archive. When promoting a specific piece, be faithful to what that piece actually is.

Current editorial series:

- **Debating Contemporary Ethical Dilemmas**: Written commentary, mostly from Ethics Olympiad-style cases, where students take positions on modern ethical problems and defend them through analogies, objections, and replies. Representative pieces: [Why Salacious Gossiping Is Wrong](https://inkstone.blog/why-salacious-gossiping-is-wrong), [The Ethics of Digital Replicas of the Deceased](https://inkstone.blog/the-ethics-of-digital-replicas-of-the-deceased), [Why and When Germline Editing is Permissible](https://inkstone.blog/why-and-when-germline-editing-is-permissible).
- **Philosophical Imaginations**: Beginner-friendly introductions to philosophical problems and thought experiments, including written explainers and short AI-assisted videos. Representative pieces: [Introducing the Extended Mind Thesis: Can the Mind be Extended Beyond the Skull?](https://inkstone.blog/introducing-the-extended-mind-thesis-can-the-mind-be-extended-beyond-the-skull), [Episode 2: Is Abortion Really Murder?](https://inkstone.blog/-ep2-j-j-thomson-s-violinist-is-abortion-really-murder-), [Episode 1: The Sorites Paradox](https://inkstone.blog/episode-1-the-sorites-paradox).

## Section 3 - Platform List and Search Strategy

### Part A - Standing platform list

Check these platforms in every outreach session, then expand based on the specific item being promoted:

- **Reddit**: Plausible for both mission-level and article-specific outreach; look for subreddits on philosophy, ethics, China/Chinese diaspora, international students, college life, writing, and the exact real-world question raised by the promoted piece. Ceiling: high for discussion fit, but rules against self-promotion are common.
- **Substack comments and community posts**: Good for thoughtful audiences around philosophy, student writing, diaspora identity, ethics, culture, and newsletters. Ceiling: medium; best when replying to a directly relevant post rather than dropping links.
- **The Student Room**: Plausible for UK/international student discussions, ethics competitions, university admissions, student life, and young writers. Ceiling: medium; stronger for student identity and education topics than for specialized philosophy.
- **Twitter/X**: Useful for finding live discussions and short replies around philosophy, AI ethics, student publishing, and Chinese student identity. Search both Latest and Top when those views are available; record age, replies, likes, reposts, and visible views. Treat Top ranking and historical reach as influence signals, not guarantees of current reply visibility. Ceiling: medium-low for deep argument; better for concise engagement and visibility.
- **Facebook groups**: Plausible for Chinese student associations, diaspora communities, international student groups, philosophy clubs, and creative writing groups. Ceiling: variable; many groups are private or have strict self-promotion rules, so check rules carefully.
- **Bluesky**: Plausible for philosophy, academia-adjacent, writing, tech ethics, and culture conversations. Search both Latest and Top when available, and compare recency with replies, reposts, and likes rather than assuming either ranking alone predicts reach. Ceiling: medium; smaller than X but often more discussion-friendly.
- **YouTube**: Use public video comments for P&P when a video's specific claim or specialist subject directly fits the promoted item. Prefer recent, active videos and relevant creators; avoid generic channel promotion or repetitive comments.
- **Effective Altruism Forum**: Plausible for applied ethics, animal ethics, AI/digital personhood, bioethics, and rigorous moral argument. Ceiling: high for a few pieces, low for site-wide promotion unless the post directly fits EA concerns.
- **LessWrong**: Plausible for philosophy of mind, AI ethics, decision theory, rationality, and cognition. Ceiling: medium-high for the extended mind, AI replica, and bioethics pieces; poor fit for general brand promotion.
- **Quora**: Can reach layperson questions about ethics, abortion arguments, gossip, AI afterlife, gene editing, and philosophy basics. Ceiling: medium for evergreen discovery, but quality varies.

### Part B - Session-specific expansion

Always combine the standing platform list with session-specific expansion. Neither alone is sufficient.

When promoting **the site as a whole**, search for communities organized around Inkstone's mission-level themes: Asian or Chinese students, diaspora identity and experience, international student life, student publishing, literary magazines, youth creative voices, student art, and cross-cultural storytelling. Starting queries:

- `Chinese student magazine submit writing`
- `Chinese diaspora student stories community`
- `international students creative writing publication`
- `Asian student literary magazine discussion`

Also search broadly for public discussions, posts, and questions about how teens or students in China spend their time: school and study, reading, creative projects, competitions, clubs, social initiatives, philosophical interests, identity, and everyday experience. These are valid mission-level targets even when there is no debate or claim to answer. The fit comes from offering direct examples of Chinese students' own work and perspectives, not from forcing the site into an argument.

When promoting **a specific article or piece**, search for communities discussing the same real-world situation, practice, or question that the piece engages with. The thread does not need to be academic. It needs to be genuinely about the same thing.

Examples:

- For **Why Salacious Gossiping Is Wrong**, good targets include layperson threads asking whether it is wrong to share a former partner's intimate secrets, whether truthful gossip can still violate privacy, or whether celebrity gossip changes the moral standard. A thread about AI-generated gossip content is not a good fit because the actual issue there is AI policy, not disclosure in intimate relationships.
- For **The Ethics of Digital Replicas of the Deceased**, good targets include communities discussing grief chatbots, digital afterlife services, posthumous data rights, or whether an AI "version" of a dead loved one should reveal secrets. A general thread about chatbot hallucination is weaker unless it specifically concerns deceased-person replicas or relational privacy.
- For **In-vitro Meat and a Relational Argument For Meat as "Morally Permissible Moral Mistakes"**, good targets include animal ethics, vegan/vegetarian debate, cultivated meat adoption, and practical arguments about whether lab-grown meat is a morally useful compromise for people unlikely to go vegan.
- For **Introducing the Extended Mind Thesis**, good targets include philosophy of mind, cognitive science, memory aids, dementia/notebook examples, phone dependence, and whether tools can become part of cognition. Layperson threads about whether phones are "part of our memory now" can qualify if the discussion is really about cognition and external tools.

## Section 4 - Reply and Affiliation Standards

Inkstone-specific disclosure should sound modest, transparent, and student-centered. Good language:

- "I help with Inkstone, a student-run platform for Chinese student writing and creative work."
- "This is from Inkstone, a student-run Chinese student publication."
- "I wrote/made a piece on Inkstone about this exact question..."

When a draft asks, invites, or suggests that someone submit writing, art, video, or another project to Inkstone, it must say that prospective contributors should write to `vold.cao@inkstone.blog`. Include the email in the draft itself; do not rely on the website link to communicate the submission route. If the draft does not mention submissions, the email is optional and normally unnecessary.

Avoid language that sounds inflated or generic, such as "leading publication," "must-read platform," "revolutionary student media," or "the definitive answer." Inkstone's public voice is earnest, reflective, creative, and student-led, not corporate or triumphalist.

P&P is the default outreach model. For an opinion-based or philosophical item, participate with an argument by engaging a concrete distinction, analogy, objection, thought experiment, inference, or conclusion. For a non-opinion specialist item, participate through a substantive explanation, interpretation, comparison, clarification, or answer tied to the target. In both cases, the contribution should remain useful without its link.

Promoting Inkstone as a platform uses the contextual website exception. Reply to an existing post, comment, question, thread, or debate that is concretely related to Chinese students' lives, changing attitudes, voices, work, or creativity. A brief acknowledgment or light extension of the target is enough before introducing Inkstone's mission-level relevance: Chinese student voices, self-representation, creative formats, and open submissions. This need not be an argument or a specialist intervention, but it must be tailored, transparent, restrained, and non-spammy. Promoting a specific item still requires the stronger opinion-based or specialist P&P standard above.
