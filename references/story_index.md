### Introducing the Extended Mind Thesis: Can the Mind be Extended Beyond the Skull?
- **URL**: https://inkstone.blog/introducing-the-extended-mind-thesis-can-the-mind-be-extended-beyond-the-skull
- **Published**: June 10, 2026
- **Author**: Vold Cao
- **Format**: written
- **Summary**: This article introduces Clark and Chalmers's Extended Mind Thesis by explaining why Otto's notebook may play the same cognitive role as Inga's biological memory. It defends the thesis against simple coupling and cognitive bloat objections by emphasizing reliability, accessibility, automatic trust, and the risk of begging the question by assuming the skin/skull boundary. - **Key concepts**: extended mind, Otto and Inga, cognitive bloat, skin and skull boundary, philosophy of mind

## Debating Contemporary Ethical Dilemmas

### Why and When Germline Editing is Permissible
- **URL**: https://inkstone.blog/why-and-when-germline-editing-is-permissible
- **Published**: June 10, 2026
- **Author**: Vold Cao
- **Format**: written
- **Summary**: This article argues that therapeutic germline editing can be morally acceptable when it prevents serious disease, even though future children cannot consent, because parents already make welfare-shaping choices before children can exercise autonomy. It replies to autonomy objections through an analogy to parental choices, replies to risk objections through double effect and risk/harm distinctions, and insists on caution, regulation, informed consent, and a sharp line against cosmetic or preference-based editing that objectifies children or encourages eugenics.
- **Key concepts**: germline editing, CRISPR, autonomy, parental choice, eugenics

### In-vitro Meat and a Relational Argument For Meat as "Morally Permissible Moral Mistakes"
- **URL**: https://inkstone.blog/in-vitro-meat-and-a-relational-argument-for-meat-as-morally-permissible-moral-mistakes-
- **Published**: June 09, 2026
- **Author**: Vold Cao
- **Format**: written
- **Summary**: This article argues that in-vitro meat is ethically better than ordinary meat because it avoids killing animals while preserving many cultural and dietary functions of meat. It criticizes a simple sentience-line argument for vegetarianism, turns instead to relation-based ethics, and treats ordinary meat-eating as suberogatory or a "morally permissible moral mistake" rather than as morally equivalent to murder. It presents lab-grown meat as a practical compromise for reducing animal suffering and environmental harm without demanding that everyone immediately adopt a vegan or vegetarian diet.
- **Key concepts**: in-vitro meat, animal ethics, relation-based ethics, vegetarianism, morally permissible moral mistake

### The Ethics of Digital Replicas of the Deceased
- **URL**: https://inkstone.blog/the-ethics-of-digital-replicas-of-the-deceased
- **Published**: June 09, 2026
- **Author**: Douglas Bai
- **Format**: written
- **Summary**: This article argues that AI replicas of the deceased are morally impermissible even with prior consent because they commodify identity, turn personhood into a data product, and cannot preserve the agency of the dead. It adds a relational privacy argument: a person's messages, recordings, and secrets involve other people who did not consent to posthumous reconstruction or disclosure. The piece rejects the idea that AI replicas are neutral truth machines, warning that they generate probabilistic narratives without the deceased person's context or defense.
- **Key concepts**: digital afterlife, AI replicas, relational privacy, consent, moral personhood

### Why Salacious Gossiping Is Wrong
- **URL**: https://inkstone.blog/why-salacious-gossiping-is-wrong
- **Published**: June 09, 2026
- **Author**: Vold Cao
- **Format**: written
- **Summary**: This article argues that sexually salacious gossip is wrong because it violates privacy, relational trust, and the implicit duties created by intimate relationships. It uses an analogy to leaking intimate footage to argue that a detailed truthful account can still breach privacy, replies to celebrity-gossip, truth-telling, and revenge objections, and concludes that Jones has a duty not to share Alice's intimate information without consent. It distinguishes ordinary private gossip, abuse-related disclosure, and sexually salacious gossip while treating the core wrong as a violation of relational contracts and moral personhood.
- **Key concepts**: salacious gossip, privacy, relational contract, truth-telling, celebrity gossip
